 Revised: Mar 22, 2016         Apollo 12 S-IVB (Spacecraft)             -399120 

 BACKGROUND:
  Launched: November 14, 1969 @ 16:22 GMT/UTC from Kennedy Space Center, USA 

  Apollo 12 was the second manned mission to land on the Moon and the sixth 
  manned mission in the United States Apollo space program. 

  It achieved a precise landing at the planned location, the site of the 
  Surveyor 3 unmanned probe which had landed April 20, 1967. Two moonwalks 
  visited Surveyor 3 and removed parts for return to Earth. Conrad and Bean 
  performed 31 hours of lunar surface activity while Gordon remained in the 
  Command Module orbiting the Moon. Apollo 12 carried the first color 
  television camera to the lunar surface. 

  The Apollo 12 astronauts returned to Earth on November 24, 1969 @ 20:58 
  GMT/UTC, the spacecraft splashing down in the Pacific Ocean, 800 km east 
  of American Samoa.

  Built by the Douglas Aircraft Company, the S-IVB served as the third stage 
  on the Saturn V. It had one J-2 engine which was fired twice.

  Apollo 12 astronauts:
   Charles "Pete" Conrad, Jr. (Commander)
   Richard F. Gordon, Jr.     (Command Module Pilot)
   Alan L. Bean               (Lunar Module Pilot)
 
 SPACECRAFT TRAJECTORY:
  The trajectory here is a reconstruction of the Apollo 12 S-IVB stage Earth 
  departure trajectory developed by Daniel R. Adamo under contract to NASA in 
  2012. The trajectory spans the time interval from 1969-Nov-14 19:33 to 
  December 06 @ 00:06 GMT/UTC.

  Propellant dumps following TLI and CSM/LM ejection targeted a lunar approach
  leading to heliocentric disposal and were performed according to plan, but 
  were based on a post-TLI trajectory that did not account for shortfall in the
  TLI delta-V.

  Consequently, the dumps over-corrected and resulted in a lunar encounter 3.4
  times higher than desired, exceeding the threshold for the lunar gravity 
  assist necessary to escape Earth-Moon space.

  The achieved S-IVB disposal trajectory therefore ended up in a gravitational 
  weak-stability region of Earth-Moon space only loosely bound to the Earth, 
  with a periapse inside the Moon's orbit.

  Over prolonged time-spans of years, small perturbations could easily have 
  drifted the Apollo 12 S-IVB stage into interplanetary/heliocentric space 
  sometime after 1976, if not before.

  To extrapolate to future times, the following heliocentric IAU76 J2000 
  ecliptic osculating elements can be manually input into Horizons as starting 
  conditions for a ballistic numerical integration using the telnet or e-mail 
  interfaces: 

  EPOCH = 2440561.50 ! A.D. 1969-Dec-06 00:00:00.0 (TDB)
   EC= 3.788077679293159E-02 QR= 9.895270525906216E-01 IN= 5.742003975916762E-02
   OM= 3.492340571440683E+01 W = 4.664715199038439E+01 Tp=  2440569.022123539820

... where

    EPOCH    Epoch Julian Day Number, Barycentric Dynamical Time
      EC     Eccentricity, e
      QR     Periapsis distance, q (au)
      IN     Inclination w.r.t xy-plane, i (degrees)
      OM     Longitude of Ascending Node, OMEGA, (degrees)
      W      Argument of Perifocus, w (degrees)
      Tp     Time of periapsis (Julian Day Number)

  Uncertainties and errors in the extrapolated position prediction will increase
  as the time from the EPOCH increases. Predictions for this man-made object 
  years past the EPOCH are problematic due to unmodeled-but-cumulative solar   
  pressure and out-gassing accelerations, yet no further tracking data to 
  characterize the forces.
