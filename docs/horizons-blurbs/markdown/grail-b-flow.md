 BACKGROUND:
  GRAIL (Gravity Recovery and Interior Laboratory) will fly twin spacecraft in 
  tandem orbits around the moon for several months to measure its gravity 
  field.  The mission is designed to answer questions about Earth's moon and 
  provide scientists a better understanding of how Earth and other rocky 
  planets in the solar system formed.

 OVERVIEW:
  * Dual spacecraft launch on a single Delta II 2920-10
  * 3.5 month low energy transfer to Moon via Earth-Sun Lagrange point (EL1)
  * Insertion maneuver separated by 25 hours
  * 50-km near-circular polar orbits with 175-225 km separation
  * 90-day science phase
  * 12-month science data analysis at end of primary mission

 OBJECTIVES:
  * Determine structure of the lunar interior, crust to core.
  * Advance understanding of thermal evolution of Moon
  * Map structure of crust and lithosphere
  * Determine subsurface structure of impact basins and origin of mascons
  * Ascertain temporal evolution of crustal brecciation and magmatism
  * Constrain deep interior structure from tides
  * Place limits on size of a possible solid core

 SPACECRAFT:
  * Rectangular composite structure
  * 2 non-articulated solar arrays
  * 3 reaction wheels
  * IMU, sun sensor, and star tracker 
  * S-band telecom for DSN
  * 22-N thruster

 INSTRUMENTS (4 packages on each spacecraft):
  * Ka-band Lunar Gravity Ranging System (LGRS), measures interspacecraft range

 TRAJECTORY:
  The GRAIL spacecraft will be launched side-by-side on a single Delta II 
  vehicle during a 26-day launch period that opens on September 8, 2011. 
  The mission was designed to avoid the December 10th, 2011 and June 4th 
  2012 lunar eclipses which interfere with the mission. 

  Both spacecraft approach the Moon under the South Pole where they execute 
  a 60-minute Lunar Orbit Insertion (LOI) maneuver to to enter an elliptical
  orbit with a period of just over 8 hours. The LOI for each of the spacecraft
  are separated by one day. 

  A series of four maneuvers are then performed to reduce the orbits to near
  circular with a mean 50-km altitude and 113-minute period. Further maneuvers 
  position the spacecraft to the desired initial separation distance, which 
  then drifts between 175 km to 225 km.

  The 90-day Science Phase is divided into three 27.3-day nadir-pointed mapping
  cycles. Two daily 8-hour DSN tracking passes acquire the science and "E/PO 
  MoonKam" data.

  Following the Science Phase (or extended mission phase), a 5-day 
  decommissioning period is planned, after which the spacecraft will impact 
  the lunar surface in ~40 days.
