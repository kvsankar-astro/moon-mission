 BACKGROUND
  Deep Space Program Science Experiment (DSPSE), better known as Clementine,
  was launched 1994-Jan-25 @ 16:34 UTC from Vandenberg Air Force Base (SLC-4W), 
  California on a Titan 2G launch vehicle.

  After launch, the spacecraft remained in a temporary parking orbit around the
  Earth until Feb. 3, 1994, at which time a solid-propellant rocket sent the 
  vehicle to the Moon. After two Earth flybys, Feb. 5 and Feb. 15, Clementine 
  successfully entered an elliptical polar orbit (about 430 x 2950 kilometers) 
  around the Moon on Feb. 19, 1994, with a period of five days.

  It departed Lunar orbit 1994-May-03 to rendevous with near-Earth asteroid 
  1620 Geographos, but an 11-minute thruster malfunction on May 7, 1994 caused
  spin-up to 80 rpm while depleting the fuel supply. The mission was
  terminated 1994-Aug-8, the spacecraft left in heliocentric space. It was
  briefly recovered in 1995.

  The BMDO assigned responsibility for the Clementine spacecraft design, 
  manufacture, integration, and mission execution to the Naval Research 
  Laboratory (NRL). The Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory (LLNL) provided 
  lightweight imaging sensors developed under the sponsorship of BMDO. 

 MISSION
  Its purpose was to map the Moon and test lightweight sensors and spacecraft
  components by exposing them to the environment of outer space. 

  Between February 26 and April 22, 1994, Clementine returned more than 
  1.8 million digital images of the Moon.
 
  Review of Clementine data indicated the possible existence of ice within some 
  of the moon's craters (South Pole). This discovery was confirmed in early 
  1998 by NASA's Lunar Prospector.
